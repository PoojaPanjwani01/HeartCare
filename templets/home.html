Heart Blockages

Heart blockages, commonly referred to as coronary artery disease (CAD), occur when the blood vessels supplying the heart with oxygen and nutrients become narrowed or blocked by the buildup of fatty deposits called plaque. This narrowing can restrict blood flow to the heart, leading to symptoms like chest pain (angina) or, in severe cases, heart attack. Effective management of heart blockages involves lifestyle changes, medications, and sometimes procedures like angioplasty or bypass surgery to restore blood flow to the heart muscle.

Heart Attack

A heart attack, also known as a myocardial infarction (MI), happens when the blood flow to a part of the heart is blocked for an extended period, usually due to a clot in one of the coronary arteries. This blockage deprives the heart muscle of oxygen and nutrients, causing the affected tissue to die. Symptoms of a heart attack can include chest pain or discomfort, shortness of breath, nausea, sweating, and lightheadedness. Prompt medical attention is crucial to minimize damage to the heart and improve the chances of survival. Treatment may involve medications, procedures like angioplasty or stent placement, and lifestyle changes to prevent future heart events.

How high Blood suger affects your heart

High blood sugar, commonly associated with diabetes, poses significant risks to heart health. It can lead to the development of atherosclerosis, a condition where plaque builds up in the arteries, narrowing them and impeding blood flow to the heart. Additionally, elevated blood sugar levels contribute to hypertension, increasing the workload on the heart and raising the risk of cardiovascular problems. Moreover, high blood sugar damages the inner lining of blood vessels, leading to endothelial dysfunction, which disrupts blood flow regulation.
